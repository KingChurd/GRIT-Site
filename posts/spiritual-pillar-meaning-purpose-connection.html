The Spiritual Pillar: Finding Meaning, Purpose, and Connection Beyond Religion

Pillar: Spiritual · ~16 min read

Let's address the elephant in the room right away: when most men hear "spiritual health," they immediately think of religion, church, or some New Age practice that doesn't resonate with them. They check out before the conversation even begins.

But here's what the research actually shows: 70% of U.S. adults consider themselves spiritual in some way, and 22% identify as "spiritual but not religious." More importantly, people who scored higher in spirituality had a higher level of mental health than those with average or low levels of spirituality — regardless of religious affiliation.

Spirituality isn't about adopting a religion or believing in a specific deity. It's about connection, meaning, purpose, and transcendence. It's about answering fundamental questions that every man faces, whether he acknowledges them or not:

  • Why am I here?
  • What gives my life meaning beyond work and survival?
  • What legacy do I want to leave?
  • How do I want to show up in the world?
  • What connects me to something larger than myself?

These aren't religious questions. They're human questions. And ignoring them doesn't make them go away—it just means you're letting them answer themselves by default rather than by design.

What Spirituality Actually Is (And Isn't)

According to the International Consensus Conference on Spiritual Care in Health Care, spirituality is "the way individuals seek ultimate meaning, purpose, connection, value, or transcendence." This could include organized religion, but it extends well beyond that to include ways of finding ultimate meaning by connecting to family, community, or nature.

Let's be clear about what we're talking about:

Spirituality IS:

  • Finding meaning and purpose in your life
  • Connecting to something larger than yourself
  • Living according to your deepest values
  • Experiencing wonder, awe, and transcendence
  • Building a coherent sense of identity and direction
  • Cultivating gratitude, compassion, and perspective
  • Understanding your place in the broader human experience

Spirituality IS NOT:

  • Necessarily religious (though it can be)
  • About believing in supernatural beings (unless you want to)
  • Requiring specific rituals or practices
  • Making you "soft" or less masculine
  • Abandoning logic or rational thinking
  • Joining a community or group (unless you choose to)
  • One-size-fits-all

A spiritually literate person is someone who reflects and cultivates skills of self-reflection, can act with prosocial orientation and altruism, can experience environmental sensitivity and kinship with nature, and is capable of astonishment and amazement at experiencing transcendence in relation to the wholeness.

Does that sound weak to you? Or does it sound like the kind of man you'd want to be?

Why Spiritual Health Matters: The Research Is Clear

You might be thinking: "This sounds nice, but is it actually important? Does it matter for my health and well-being?"

The answer, backed by extensive research, is an unequivocal yes.

Spirituality and Mental Health

Spirituality should be incorporated into care for both serious illness and overall health, according to a study led by researchers at Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health, which represents the most rigorous and comprehensive systematic analysis of modern day literature regarding health and spirituality.

The research on spirituality and mental health is compelling:

  • Having greater purpose in life was significantly associated with lower levels of depression and anxiety, and people with greater purpose in life experienced less stress.
  • Individuals with a greater purpose in life have better psycho-physical health throughout the life span.
  • People with the greatest wellbeing (which includes sense of meaning and purpose) were 30% less likely to die during an eight and a half year follow-up period than those with the least wellbeing.

Think about that last one. Having a sense of meaning and purpose in your life is associated with a 30% lower risk of death. That's not a small effect. That's massive.

Purpose as Protection

Purpose in life has been found to be a big predictor of mortality, with one study showing that if you have greater purpose in life across all age groups from 20 to 70-year-olds, you're less likely to be dead 10 years later.

Purpose acts as a psychological buffer. When you have a clear sense of why you're here and what matters to you, you're more resilient in the face of adversity. You bounce back faster from setbacks. You make healthier choices because they align with your larger goals. You have a reason to take care of yourself.

Victor Frankl, a Holocaust concentration camp survivor who lost his family yet survived and continued his work as a psychiatrist, stressed the importance of finding meaning even in suffering. His survival and continued contribution is perhaps the most profound example of how purpose provides resilience to extreme adversity.

The Connection Between Meaning and Well-Being

People who have meaning and purpose in their lives are happier, and finding purpose creates sustainable well-being. This isn't just feel-good rhetoric — it's backed by rigorous research.

Happiness is more about feeling good and comfort in life, whereas well-being is far more comprehensive, with meaning and purpose being really important. You can be happy in the moment without well-being, and you can have deep meaning without constant happiness. But together, they create something powerful: a life worth living.

We derive meaning and purpose from our social networks, families, values, cultures, employment, and environments. The process of cultivating that sense of meaning may not always make you happy in the moment, but it creates something more durable: fulfillment.

The Four Dimensions of Spiritual Health

Based on research and practical application, spiritual health can be understood through four interconnected dimensions. You don't need to master all four simultaneously — but understanding them gives you a framework for developing this pillar.

1. Connection to Self: Self-Awareness and Reflection

This is about understanding who you are beneath the roles you play — not just what you do, but who you are.

Most men spend their entire lives moving from one task to the next, one goal to the next, without ever pausing to ask fundamental questions about their identity, values, and desires. They know what they should do, but they've never examined why they're doing it or whether it aligns with who they truly want to be.

Practical self-connection:

Develop a reflection practice. This doesn't have to be meditation (though it can be). It could be:

  • Journaling for 10 minutes each morning or evening.
  • Taking a weekly "thinking walk" where you process your week.
  • Regular check-ins: "How am I feeling? What's really going on?"
  • Annual reviews where you examine your year and plan the next.

Know your values. Write down your top 5–7 core values — the principles that guide your decisions. Examples: integrity, growth, family, service, creativity, freedom, courage. Then examine whether your daily life reflects these values. Where are you out of alignment?

Understand your story. How did you become who you are? What experiences shaped you? What patterns keep repeating? Understanding your narrative helps you author your future intentionally rather than reactively.

Practice self-compassion. This isn't about lowering standards — it's about treating yourself with the same kindness you'd show a good friend. Men are often brutally hard on themselves in ways that undermine rather than motivate growth.

2. Connection to Others: Relationships and Community

Humans are fundamentally social beings. We're not meant to do life alone. Yet many men find themselves isolated, with surface-level relationships that don't meet their deeper needs for authentic connection.

Spiritual health includes recognizing that you're part of a larger human tapestry — that your life is interwoven with others in ways that matter.

Practical relational connection:

Cultivate depth, not just breadth. You don't need 50 friends. You need 3–5 people you can be real with. People who know your struggles, your dreams, your fears. People you don't have to perform for.

Practice vulnerability. Real connection requires letting people see you — not just your highlight reel, but your actual experience. This feels risky because it is. But it's also how meaningful relationships are built.

Serve others. Research shows that people who report greater meaning in life are better relationship partners, neighbors, and citizens. Contributing to something beyond yourself — whether through volunteering, mentoring, or simply being present for people who need you — builds spiritual health.

Build or join a community. This could be a men's group, a sports team, a volunteer organization, a faith community (if that resonates), or any group united by shared values or interests. Humans need to belong to something larger than themselves.

3. Connection to Nature: Environmental and Existential Awareness

Nature provides a unique window into spirituality that exists outside conventional religion. It's where many men naturally experience transcendence, perspective, and peace — even if they don't label it as "spiritual."

The research on nature and mental health is overwhelming:

People who spent two hours a week in green spaces were substantially more likely to report good health and psychological well-being than those who don't. Two hours was identified as a threshold — there were no benefits for people who didn't meet it.

As little as 10 to 20 minutes in nature daily has been shown to help prevent stress and mental health strain. Nature lowers stress hormones like cortisol, helps slow heart rate and blood pressure, boosts mood through increased serotonin and dopamine, and invites experiences of awe.

Time around nature decreases symptoms of anxiety and depression, strengthens wellbeing, and lowers stress. It also reduces loneliness and increases feelings of connection and belonging.

Why Nature Matters for Spirituality

Nature provides perspective. When you're standing at the base of a mountain, watching the ocean, or walking through an old-growth forest, your problems don't disappear — but they're recontextualized. You're reminded that you're part of something vast and ancient. Your ego quiets down. Your awareness expands.

Nature provides windows on spirituality that exist outside conventional religion and current science, despite having been vital in the history of our species. For most of human history, our spiritual experiences were intimately tied to the natural world. We've only recently become disconnected from this source.

Practical nature connection:

Make nature time non-negotiable. Aim for that two-hour weekly minimum. This could be:

  • Weekend hikes or bike rides.
  • Fishing, hunting, or camping.
  • Trail running or outdoor workouts.
  • Gardening or yard work done mindfully.
  • Simply sitting outside without your phone.

Practice forest bathing or nature immersion. Originating from Japan, forest bathing involves immersing yourself in the forest atmosphere. This practice has been associated with lowered heart rate and blood pressure, reduced stress hormone production, and improved overall feelings of wellbeing.

Engage your senses. Don't just be in nature — be present in it. Notice the smells, sounds, textures, and sights. This sensory engagement deepens the experience and amplifies its benefits.

Bring nature indoors. Even if you live in an urban environment, houseplants, nature photos, and natural light can provide some benefits. But prioritize getting outside whenever possible.

4. Connection to Purpose: Meaning, Mission, and Legacy

This is the core of spiritual health: understanding why you're here and what you're working toward beyond survival and comfort.

Purpose in life means having a central, organizing life aim, an overall sense of direction, and a belief that one's life activities are valuable and important — making a positive mark on the world.

More than half of young adults (58%) reported they lacked meaning or purpose in their lives, and half said their mental health was negatively influenced by not knowing what to do with their life. This isn't just an existential problem — it's a health crisis.

The Difference Between Goals and Purpose

Goals are specific, time-bound, and achievable: "Get promoted," "Run a marathon," "Buy a house." They're important, but they're not purpose.

Purpose is broader and more enduring: it's the direction your life is pointed, the kind of person you want to become, the impact you want to have. Goals serve your purpose; purpose gives goals meaning.

Finding Your Purpose

This isn't something you discover in a weekend workshop. It's an ongoing process of exploration, experimentation, and refinement. Here's how to approach it:

Examine your experiences:

  • When have you felt most alive and engaged?
  • What activities make you lose track of time?
  • What would you do even if you weren't paid?
  • What injustices or problems make you angry or sad?
  • What do people consistently come to you for?

Consider your values and strengths:

  • What matters most to you?
  • What are you naturally good at?
  • Where do your skills and passions intersect with the world's needs?

Think about impact and legacy:

  • What do you want to be remembered for?
  • What difference do you want to make?
  • Who do you want to help or serve?
  • What would you regret not doing?

Start with "be-goals".

Instead of just setting achievement goals, develop "be-goals" — a developmental blueprint connecting the essence of who you want to be with purposeful actions. Not just "I want to earn $X" but "I want to be someone who provides security and opportunities for my family." Not just "I want to get in shape" but "I want to be someone who models health and vitality for my kids."

Your purpose will evolve.

Purpose in life is really individual and can be as simple as having someone who needs you, like your children, a pet, career volunteer work, or a goal of spreading well-being and kindness.

Your purpose at 25 might look different from your purpose at 45 or 65. That's normal and healthy. The key is always having an answer to "Why am I here?" and "What am I working toward?"

Building Your Spiritual Practice: A Practical Framework

Unlike physical health (go to the gym) or even mental health (go to therapy), spiritual health doesn't have an obvious starting point. Here's a framework to build your practice:

Month 1: Assessment and Exploration

Week 1: Self-assessment

Rate yourself (1–10) in each spiritual dimension:

  • Connection to self: How well do I know myself?
  • Connection to others: How deep and meaningful are my relationships?
  • Connection to nature: How often am I outside and present in natural spaces?
  • Connection to purpose: How clear am I on why I'm here and what I'm building?

Be honest. Most men will score low in at least one area, and that's okay. This is your baseline.

Week 2: Exploration

Try one practice from each dimension:

  • Self: Journal for 10 minutes.
  • Others: Have one vulnerable conversation.
  • Nature: Spend 30 minutes outside without your phone.
  • Purpose: Write one page on "What matters most to me."

Notice what resonates and what feels uncomfortable (uncomfortable often means important).

Week 3: Research and learning

  • Read one book on meaning or purpose (recommendations below).
  • Listen to podcasts about topics that interest you spiritually.
  • Explore different practices: meditation, breathwork, nature immersion, volunteering.

Week 4: Choose your focus

Based on your assessment, pick the dimension that needs the most attention. This will be your primary focus for the next 2–3 months.

Months 2–3: Building Consistency

Week 5–12: Daily practice

Choose ONE daily practice (10–20 minutes):

  • For self-connection: morning journaling or evening reflection.
  • For relational connection: reach out to one person meaningfully each day.
  • For nature connection: daily walk outside, mindfully observing your surroundings.
  • For purpose connection: spend time on work that aligns with your purpose.

The key is consistency over intensity. Better to do 10 minutes daily than 2 hours once a month.

Weekly check-in:

Every Sunday, reflect:

  • Did I do my daily practice most days?
  • What did I notice or learn this week?
  • What felt good? What felt hard?
  • What needs adjustment?

Months 4–6: Integration and Expansion

Add a second practice.

Once your first practice is solidly habitual (you do it without much resistance), add a second dimension.

Monthly deep dive:

Once a month, do something more substantial:

  • A day-long solo hike for nature connection.
  • An honest conversation about your relationship for relational connection.
  • A full-day reflection retreat for self-connection.
  • Volunteer work aligned with your purpose.

Review and refine:

Every 3 months, reassess where you are. How have your scores changed? What's working? What needs more attention?

Common Obstacles (And How to Overcome Them)

"This Feels Weird or Awkward"

Of course it does. You're doing something new that challenges years of conditioning that told you to be productive, not reflective; to act, not feel; to achieve, not connect.

Solution: start small and private. You don't have to tell anyone you're journaling or meditating or thinking about purpose. Make it an experiment: "I'll try this for 30 days and see what happens."

"I Don't Have Time"

You have time for what you prioritize. If you have time to scroll social media, watch TV, or sit in traffic ruminating, you have time for this.

Solution: start with 10 minutes. Set a timer. Anyone can find 10 minutes. As the benefits become clear, you'll naturally want to invest more time.

"This Doesn't Seem Masculine"

Self-reflection, connection, meaning, and purpose aren't masculine or feminine — they're human. The strongest men throughout history have been those with a clear sense of who they are and why they're here.

Solution: reframe strength. Real strength includes the courage to examine your life, the wisdom to seek meaning, and the discipline to live according to your values. That's not soft — that's advanced.

"I Don't Know Where to Start"

That's why this article exists. Pick one thing from one dimension and start there.

Solution: if completely overwhelmed, start with nature. Just a few minutes (1–10 minutes) of exposure to nature can boost attention, reduce feelings of stress, and improve your mood. It's the most accessible entry point for many men.

"I'm Not Religious and This Feels Religious"

Spirituality ≠ religion. 70% of Americans consider themselves spiritual in some way, and 22% identify as "spiritual but not religious". You can develop spiritual health entirely outside any religious framework.

Solution: use language that resonates with you. Instead of "spiritual practice," call it "personal development" or "life design" or "purpose work." The label doesn't matter; the practice does.

Resources to Explore

Books:

  • Man's Search for Meaning by Viktor Frankl.
  • The Obstacle Is the Way by Ryan Holiday.
  • Awareness by Anthony de Mello.
  • When Things Fall Apart by Pema Chödrön.
  • The Nature Fix by Florence Williams.

Practices to explore:

  • Meditation and mindfulness (apps: Headspace, Calm, Waking Up).
  • Breathwork (Wim Hof Method, Box Breathing).
  • Journaling (Morning Pages, Evening Reflection, Gratitude).
  • Nature immersion (Forest Bathing, hiking, outdoor meditation).
  • Stoic philosophy (Daily Stoic, Meditations by Marcus Aurelius).
  • Volunteer work (mentoring, community service).

Communities:

  • Men's groups (Sacred Sons, The ManKind Project, local groups).
  • Outdoor communities (hiking groups, adventure clubs).
  • Service organizations (volunteer opportunities).
  • Practice communities (meditation groups, philosophy circles).

The Bottom Line: Your Life Needs More Than Success

You can have money, status, fitness, relationships, and still feel empty if you haven't addressed the spiritual dimension. You can achieve every goal and still wonder "What's the point?"

Hundreds of scientific studies have firmly linked meaning in life to lesser suffering and greater wellbeing, both psychologically and physically. This isn't optional. It's foundational.

The spiritual pillar isn't about becoming someone else. It's about becoming more fully yourself. It's about living intentionally rather than reactively. It's about building a life that feels meaningful, not just successful.

It's about answering the questions that every man faces but few address:

  • Who am I beyond what I do?
  • What do I stand for?
  • What gives my life meaning?
  • What legacy am I building?
  • How do I want to show up in the world?

These questions don't go away when you ignore them. They just answer themselves by default — through drift, through cultural conditioning, through unconscious patterns inherited from your upbringing.

The alternative is to answer them intentionally. To design your life rather than just live it. To choose your values rather than absorb them. To cultivate meaning rather than hope it appears.

That's what spiritual health is. And it matters just as much as your physical fitness and mental clarity.

Maybe more.
